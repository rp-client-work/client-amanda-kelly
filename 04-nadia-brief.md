# Nadia — Offer & Psychology Brief (Home + About)

## PART 1 — OFFER STRUCTURE

**Framing across three audiences (one brand, three access points, not three different products):**

- **Retail buyers:** framed as choosing a *texture and configuration*, not "buying hair." Packaging language centers on precision (named texture, length, bundle/closure/frontal combination) so the purchase feels like a specification, the way you'd spec a made-to-order garment. CTA: "SHOP THE TEXTURES."
- **Working stylists:** framed as a professional relationship, not a one-off purchase. The offer is behavioral/technical reliability across repeat installs and color services, positioned as something the stylist can build a client relationship on. CTAs: "BOOK FOR THE CHAIR" / "SEE STYLIST PRICING." Packaging: stylist pricing tier is presented as recognition of professional use-case, not a discount clearance.
- **Wholesale/salon-boutique accounts:** framed as a supply relationship built on consistency and disclosure. Packaging: minimums, samples, and sourcing transparency are presented as terms of a serious business partnership, not a bulk discount hustle. CTA: "REQUEST WHOLESALE ACCESS."

**Risk reversal (white-glove/consultative, not discount-store):**
Since intake bans false urgency and status-transformation, and bans discount framing, guarantees must be reframed as *process transparency* rather than "money-back if unhappy":
- Retail: pre-purchase texture guidance (e.g., named texture descriptions precise enough that buyers can self-match with confidence) functions as the risk reversal — the uncertainty is handled before the sale, not refunded after it. Raegan can render this as an implicit confidence-builder in the "SIX TEXTURES. ONE STANDARD." grid and FAQ, not a bolted-on guarantee badge.
- Stylists: the "FOR THE CHAIR" section itself *is* the risk reversal — proactively answering color-processing and tangle-across-installs behavior removes the stylist's actual professional risk (a client complaint mid-service) before they ever ask.
- Wholesale: origin transparency and stated consistency standards (single-donor, cuticle-aligned) function as the trust mechanism replacing a generic guarantee; samples (mentioned in "FOR THE CASE") are the literal try-before-you-commit mechanism.
- Do not add a manufactured guarantee badge, countdown, or "30-day money back" if it isn't confirmed real; none is specified in intake, so none is invented here. If the client has an actual return/consultation policy, Raegan should ask before implying one.

**Value stacking:**
The "stack" is the construction detail itself, not bundled freebies: single-donor sourcing + cuticle intact/aligned + hand-tied/hand-picked construction + precisely matched texture naming, all delivered as one integrated standard rather than upsell add-ons. Closures/frontals are framed as matched extensions of the same standard, not a separate upsell category. This stacking logic should visibly build claim-by-claim through Home's pillars section and black band before any CTA appears.

**Urgency/scarcity:**
None fabricated. The only legitimate scarcity mechanic available in the material given is single-donor sourcing itself (a single donor's hair is inherently a finite, non-mass-manufactured supply) — this can be mentioned as a factual attribute of sourcing, never dressed up as "only 3 left" or countdown urgency. Wholesale minimums are a real business mechanic, not manufactured urgency, and should be stated plainly as terms, not pressure.

Objections used below are the three explicitly flagged for this project: (1) raw-vs-virgin authenticity/origin skepticism (retail + general), (2) color-processing and tangle behavior across installs (stylist), (3) origin transparency, consistency, and minimums (wholesale). Q19 (comparison-intent keyword, FAQ-only, competitor-silent per Jasmin) is also carried through as an objection-handling instance.

## Page: Home

**Section order per Jasmin's outline:** Hero → "THE DIFFERENCE IS IN THE SOURCING" (pillars) → "SIX TEXTURES. ONE STANDARD." → black band "THE CONSTRUCTION" → "CLOSURES AND FRONTALS TO MATCH" → brand quote → two-column "FOR THE CHAIR"/"FOR THE CASE" → testimonials "IN THEIR WORDS" → FAQ → final CTA.

**Influence principles by section (specific, not blanket):**
- Hero: **consistency/commitment** — "named and traced" sets an expectation of specificity the rest of the page must keep proving; this is a promise the visitor mentally holds you to, not a persuasion push itself.
- "THE DIFFERENCE IS IN THE SOURCING": **authority** — named, specific process detail (single-donor, cuticle intact, hand-tied) functions as expertise-signaling authority, replacing the vague "100% human hair" claim competitors lean on. This is the page's primary authority beat.
- "SIX TEXTURES. ONE STANDARD.": **consistency** — demonstrates the named-precision promise from the hero is applied uniformly across all six textures, reinforcing the earlier commitment rather than introducing a new tactic.
- Black band "THE CONSTRUCTION": **authority** continued, deepened into visible mechanism (what hand-tied actually means) — this is where skepticism about "virgin" claims gets defused with specificity instead of assertion.
- "CLOSURES AND FRONTALS TO MATCH": **consistency** — extends the standard rather than opening a new claim, keep it low-key.
- Brand quote block: **liking** — a single humanizing brand-voice moment; use sparingly, this is the only "liking" beat on the page.
- "FOR THE CHAIR" / "FOR THE CASE": **social proof by proxy + loss aversion** — segment-specific objection handling framed around what the stylist/buyer stands to lose (a mid-service complaint, an inconsistent shipment) if unaddressed, resolved by process detail.
- "IN THEIR WORDS": **social proof**, straightforward, the page's only real social-proof beat — do not front-load testimonials earlier, they land better after the process case has already been made on its own merits (testimony should confirm the claims, not carry them).
- FAQ: **authority + reciprocity** — proactively answering real buyer questions is a reciprocity move (giving useful information before being asked builds goodwill) layered on the authority already established.
- Final CTA "FIND YOUR TEXTURE": **consistency** close — invites the visitor to act in line with the specificity they've been shown, not a scarcity-driven push.

**Objection-handling sequence (in page order):**
1. Raw-vs-virgin authenticity/origin skepticism — surfaced implicitly in Hero, answered directly and specifically in "THE DIFFERENCE IS IN THE SOURCING" and deepened in "THE CONSTRUCTION." This must be resolved before the page ever asks for stylist- or wholesale-specific trust.
2. Color-processing/tangle behavior across installs (stylist objection) — held until "FOR THE CHAIR," not addressed earlier; introducing it too early would import a service-specific worry into a page section meant for general buyers.
3. Origin transparency/consistency/minimums (wholesale objection) — held until "FOR THE CASE," same logic.
4. Comparison-intent skepticism (Q19-type "how is this different") — must NOT be answered by contrasting with competitors anywhere on Home; if it surfaces implicitly (a visitor wondering "why should I believe this over any other site"), the page's answer is entirely the specificity of the sourcing/construction sections, never a direct comparison. Flag to Raegan: do not let this drift into "unlike other brands" language anywhere on this page.
5. General residual skepticism/deferred practical questions — swept into FAQ as the last full defusal before the final CTA.

**Emotional arc (mapped to actual sections):**
Hero (confident, specific claim, no hype) → pillars section (curiosity resolving into credibility as sourcing/construction specifics land) → texture grid (a settling-in, "there's something for me specifically" recognition) → construction black band (deepened respect/reassurance, the page's trust peak) → closures/frontals (calm continuation, no new tension) → brand quote (a warm, human breath before the segmented asks) → chair/case columns (targeted relevance: stylist and wholesale visitors each feel individually spoken to, tension of their specific unaddressed risk raised and then resolved in the same block) → testimonials (external validation, tension release) → FAQ (last practical doubts cleared, calm) → final CTA (an invited, low-pressure decision, not a manufactured close).

**Trust-building beats before the ask:**
Before the final CTA lands, the page must have already established: (1) specific, named sourcing/construction detail (not adjectives), (2) demonstrated consistency of that standard across all six textures, (3) segment-specific proof the stylist/wholesale visitor's real professional risk has been considered, (4) third-party testimonial confirmation, (5) FAQ closure on any remaining practical question. All five must land before "FIND YOUR TEXTURE" — the CTA is a closing gesture, not a persuasion event of its own.

## Page: About

**Section order per Jasmin's outline:** Hero ("About AmandaKelly Co." / "100% Virgin Hair Extensions") → "WHY WE STARTED" (Origin) → black band "WHAT WE STAND FOR" (pillars) → "WHO WE SERVE" (Retail/Chair/Case, each addressing that segment's objection) → Sister Brand block (untouched) → FAQ → reviews band → final CTA.

**Influence principles by section:**
- Hero: plain statement of identity, no persuasion tactic needed or appropriate here — a name plate, not a pitch.
- "WHY WE STARTED": **unity** — origin story is where shared identity/values with the audience is built (why this business exists, what it was built to solve), the one section on the whole site suited to a unity appeal. Must stay strictly on provenance/craftsmanship motivation per intake, never a status or transformation narrative.
- Black band "WHAT WE STAND FOR": **authority + consistency** — same as Home's sourcing section, restated as institutional principle rather than product claim; this is the About-page version of proving the hero's claim is structurally true, not just a hero flourish.
- "WHO WE SERVE": **social proof by relevance + loss aversion** — each of the three sub-sections (Retail/Chair/Case) should read as "we already understand people like you and what could go wrong for you," directly defusing that segment's real objection.
- Sister Brand block: no tactic applied, out of scope, left as-is.
- FAQ: **reciprocity + authority**, same logic as Home.
- Reviews band: **social proof**, the About page's equivalent of Home's testimonials, placed later (right before the CTA) since About's persuasion job is trust-building through story rather than product-feature proof, so social proof works best as a final confirming beat rather than a mid-page one.
- Final CTA "FIND YOUR TEXTURE": **consistency** close, same logic as Home.

**Objection-handling sequence (in page order):**
1. Raw-vs-virgin authenticity/origin skepticism — implicitly addressed in "WHY WE STARTED" (why sourcing matters to the founders) and made explicit in "WHAT WE STAND FOR."
2. Segment-specific objections deferred to "WHO WE SERVE": retail's authenticity concern re-confirmed briefly, stylist's color-processing/tangle-behavior objection addressed in that sub-section, wholesale's origin-transparency/consistency/minimums objection addressed in that sub-section. Order within "WHO WE SERVE" should follow Jasmin's stated order (Retail, then Chair, then Case) since that already sequences from broadest to most specialized audience.
3. Comparison-intent skepticism (Q19) — FAQ only, competitor-silent, same restriction as Home. Flag to Raegan explicitly.
4. Residual doubts — FAQ, last defusal before reviews and CTA.

**Emotional arc (mapped to actual sections):**
Hero (calm, declarative identity) → "WHY WE STARTED" (warmth, sincerity, the emotional high point of the page: this is where the brand becomes a "someone," not a product) → "WHAT WE STAND FOR" (warmth cooling into credibility, principles stated with rigor) → "WHO WE SERVE" (targeted recognition for each segment, same beat structure as Home's chair/case columns but slower and more narrative since About's voice is more personal) → Sister Brand (neutral, out of emotional arc, brief) → FAQ (settling, practical) → reviews (external confirmation, quiet confidence) → final CTA ("SEE THE PROCESS" as soft-CTA earlier in the page per Jasmin, "FIND YOUR TEXTURE" as the true close: low-pressure invitation, consistent with the calm register established throughout).

**Trust-building beats before the ask:**
Before either CTA lands, the page needs: (1) a genuine, specific origin motivation (not generic "we love hair" sentiment — tie to provenance/craftsmanship per intake), (2) institutional restatement of the same named claims from Home (so About isn't a redundant retelling but a deepening), (3) segment-specific recognition in "WHO WE SERVE," (4) FAQ closure, (5) reviews as final social validation. Soft-CTA "SEE THE PROCESS" can appear earlier (logically after "WHAT WE STAND FOR," inviting deeper engagement with Home's construction detail) without needing the full trust stack, since it's a lower-commitment ask than the final CTA.

## Flags for Raegan (drafting-stage guardrails)

- **Comparison drift risk:** Q19 and any "how are you different" framing must be answered by naming AmandaKelly's own process only. Never write "unlike other brands," "not like beauty supply hair," or any durability-contest phrasing, on either page, in FAQ or body copy.
- **Status/transformation drift risk:** the origin story ("WHY WE STARTED") and brand quote block are the two spots most tempting to slip into "how the hair makes you feel/look" language given their emotional register. Keep both anchored to provenance/craftsmanship motivation, not self-image or transformation.
- **Longevity drift risk:** durability/longevity claims may only appear inside FAQ answers, never in section headers, pillar copy, or the hero. If a construction detail (hand-tied, cuticle-aligned) naturally implies durability, let the implication sit unstated in body sections; only make it explicit if and where it's already inside an FAQ answer.
- **Urgency drift risk:** do not add countdown timers, fake stock counters, or "today only" language anywhere; single-donor sourcing may be mentioned as a factual finite-supply attribute but never dramatized into manufactured urgency.
